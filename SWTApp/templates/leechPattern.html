Fly Patterns Imitating Leeches

Leech Patterns: Undulating movement

Trout can count on leeches. Never a big hatch or massive emergence, but theres always a few leeches around. It's really common to find a leech inside a trout. How do you imitate a leech? Really it's one of the easier naturals to mimic. The color range includes black, brown, olive and maroon but black and olive are most common. The body needs to be that longish shape of course. If there is a problem its imitating the undulating swimming motion of the leech. A soft hair or hackle body will give it some life and a bead on one end or the other will make the movement erratic causing the fly to rise and dive as you retrieve.

This is a micro M.O.A.L (mother of all leeches) leech pattern. Borrowed from steelheading and scaled down for trout its a great pattern. The hook is an octopus style connected to a shank with bead by some gelspun line. The body is made by spinning dubbing into the gel spun. The pattern is soft and furry and limp. Retrieving this fly will cause it to rise and pausing will allow the beaded head to fall all the while the soft body follows the lead.

Micro leeches fished under an indicator are always a good bet when there is no other hatch.

Really small micro leeches work better than big ones.

This pattern has a bead on the stinger hook and a hook clipped at the bend for a body. The two pieces are connected by gelspun.

If you're packing a survival kit for a bushplane a few rootbeer microleeches can keep you alive until the search party finds you.
